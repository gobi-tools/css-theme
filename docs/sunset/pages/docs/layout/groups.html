    • Some elements are visually meant to stick together. In such a case, you can wrap them in a parent that's been given the group role.

    In the case of a group of buttons, all horizontal spacing and borders between them dissapear.

    <p role="group">
  <button>
    Option 1
  </button>f
  <button type="reset">
    Option 2
  </button>
</p>

    In the case of a group of marks, they're also pulled together and have any vertical space dissapear.

    #testv1.0.0

    <p role="group">
  <mark>
    #test
  </mark>
  <mark 
    class="success">
    v1.0.0
  </mark>
</p>

    Grouping elements really shines in the case of forms and form inputs. You can see below an example of a compact login form.

    <form>
  <div role="group">
    <input 
      id="email" 
      type="email" 
      placeholder="Email"/>
    <input 
      id="password" 
      type="password" 
      placeholder="Password"/>
    <input 
      type="submit" 
      value={"Login"}/>
  </div>
</form>

    Grouping elements can be used to style icons and text together.

    Test Address, SE11 8CL
    <div role="group">
  <svg 
    width="20" 
    height="20" ...>
  </svg>
  <b>
    Test Address, SE11 8CL
  </b>
</div>

    Other elements, such as images, can also be grouped, although the impact isn't as pronounced.

    <p role="group">
  <img 
    width="80" 
    height="80" 
    src="..." 
    alt="image 1"/>
  <img 
    width="80" 
    height="80" 
    src="..." 
    alt="image 2"/>
</p>
